LIFE

She was born on June 9, 1922, in Florence, in the house on Via Caselli, at the corner with Via Cento Stelle near Campo di Marte. Later, she moved with her parents to Via Ximenes (named after a mathematician-astronomer) near the hill of Arcetri, the site of the current astrophysical observatory (and the village of Arcetri where Galileo spent his last years, under "house arrest" in the villa Il Gioiello).
The elements that make it up are positive, the activities, and negative, the liabilities.

Parents

Her father, Roberto Hack, was of Swiss origin, Protestant, worked as an accountant and her mother, Catholic, a teacher and graduate of the Academy of Fine Arts, was a miniaturist at the Uffizi Gallery. Neither of them was satisfied with their religions, so they embraced theosophy: her parents always allowed her the freedom to believe in what she wanted. She never believed in reincarnation. She didn't worry about death, but she did about illness. "I'm afraid of suffering, of not being autonomous anymore, that's why I'm so in favor of euthanasia. Life and death belong to man and not to God, in my opinion."

Youth

During the fascist period, they suffered heavy discrimination for not joining the party. She lived most of her youth under the dictatorship of fascism: her family was affected by fascist laws because her father, employed at Valdarno - the Tuscan electric company - was fired for not joining the fascist party. Her mother for many years was the only support of the family selling to tourists the miniatures she made at the Uffizi.

Repercussions of Fascism

As a girl, she didn't know what fascism was until 1938 (enactment of racial laws): she had an excellent science teacher who was Jewish; from one day to the next she no longer came to school. They tried to find out, to know what had happened to her... then she realized what fascism really meant. Only after the war did they learn that she had been imprisoned in Santa Verdiana, the women's prison in Florence, and that twenty days later she had committed suicide: she had poisoned herself.

In May 1940, arguing in class with some fascist comrades, she had proclaimed herself against entering the war. She was reported by the principal, labeled as "a defeatist" and anti-fascist. She risked losing the entire year and being expelled from all schools, but the professors' council managed to obtain only a one-month suspension and punished with a seven in conduct. She was supposed to go in October with all subjects, with that seven in conduct, but Italy's entry into the war On June 10, 1940, the abolition of graduation exams and she passed with the vote by scrutiny. "It's strange to think that I got through thanks to the Second World War."

Choice of Faculty

She enrolled in Letters because it was the only faculty she had heard of, and she had a lot of ease in writing, but she only stayed there for an hour: it was boring and she realized that she was more cut out for deeds than for chatter, so she decided to leave, and only then did she remember that physics was the subject she liked the most and enrolled in that faculty. Marriage with Aldo

In February 1944, she married Aldo: she had no desire to get married, she considered marriage useless. "In church, then! I was ashamed like a dog." But Aldo's parents were religious. It was a very simple ceremony, there were about eight people. No wedding lunch. They went alone to eat at a trattoria.

They had known each other since they were children, in Florence: she was eleven and he was thirteen, they met and spent time playing together. They didn't see each other for 10 years: Aldo's father was transferred for work. They met again at university but didn't like each other very much: "We always argued, and I don't remember how it ended up that we fell in love and even got married, also because we're really different: I'm an atheist. He was Catholic, I am a person of science and he a literary, a living encyclopedia, I am combative, he too but unpredictable, a dreamer, like an extraterrestrial." They never had children, she believed that there are those who are meant to be parents, and those who are not, and she said she was not inclined and besides, he was never interested in becoming a mother.

First Work Experiences

Her first real job was offered to her in 1947 by Ducati in Milan, which produced photographic machines. She accepted and moved there, but the following year she preferred to return to the Florentine university environment, as she was offered the position of assistant appointed. After the Milanese period, the Arcetri Observatory became almost her second home.

In 1950, she won the competition for the position of assistant professor in the astronomy department. In 1964, she had her first job in Trieste for the astronomy department at the Institute of Theoretical Physics of the University of Trieste. She rebuilt an almost nonexistent Observatory and founded a school, where she was director. In 1997, she retired and began discovering new hobbies like volleyball, cycling, and swimming.

Ideologies

She has always been a vegetarian, as her parents were too. "Meat disgusts me, fish even more. I can't stand the idea of the suffering of the animal that dies, of life being broken to become a dish." "Knowledge makes us free, allows us to understand who governs us, to understand what is good and what is bad. As Dario Fo says, the worker knows a hundred words and the industrialist knows a thousand, that's why the industrialist always tricks the worker. So I will always fight to support research, to support the study of astronomy and other fields."
